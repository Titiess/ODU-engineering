## Reflections

Building a government digital service taught lessons that apply to any production system, but with higher stakes.

### Architecture Over Features

The most important work on this project was not building features — it was designing the right architecture. The state-machine model for application processing, the separation of citizen and admin interfaces, and the webhook-based payment confirmation pattern were decisions made in the first weeks that influenced every subsequent piece of work.

### Security Is a Foundation, Not a Feature

Government systems handle sensitive citizen data. Security cannot be an afterthought or a sprint task. On this project, security-first decisions (encrypted storage, JWT rotation, role-based access) were established as architectural constraints before any feature work began.

### The Value of Defensive Engineering

In production, the edge cases dominate. What happens when a payment webhook arrives twice? What happens when a citizen uploads an invalid document format? What happens when the Chairman rejects an application that has already been paid for?

Every one of these scenarios required specific handling. The discipline of anticipating and engineering for failure cases is what separates production software from prototypes.

### Government Workflows Are Complex

Digitising a government process is not simply moving a paper form online. It requires understanding approval hierarchies, audit requirements, exception handling, and the political dynamics of institutional change. The technology is often the simpler part of the problem.

### User Experience Drives Adoption

Citizens who had always visited a government office in person needed to trust a digital alternative. The platform's usability — clear progress indicators, immediate payment confirmation, accessible certificate downloads — directly influenced adoption rates.
